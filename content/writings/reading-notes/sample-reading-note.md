My brother sent me this article, as he usually does with most interesting AI articles and X threads, but I decided to actually take the time to take notes and reflect on this one. 
The piece is about a problem I hadn't really thought about before: we got extraordinarily lucky that modern AI reasons out loud. When chain-of-thought prompting emerged, which is essentially asking models to "show their work", it accidentally made AI cognition visible in plain English for the first time. Instead of smarter models becoming bigger black boxes, they became more transparent. In this article, the author argues that that luck is running out.
The core tension is between what's useful for humans to read and what's useful for a model to compute. Chain-of-thought reasoning isn't communication, it's actually scratch paper that happens to be in English. There's no evolutionary pressure keeping it legible to us, only pressure toward whatever helps the model get the right answer. The author draws an analogy to language drift. Basically, Old English took a millennium to become unreadable to us, but gradient descent moves much faster than mouths do. AI reasoning could drift into semi-coherent "Thinkish", which is already documented in current models, or skip language entirely in favor of high-dimensional internal states that no human could parse at all.
Even when models show their reasoning, it often doesn't reflect what's actually happening computationally. Models can ignore corrupted reasoning traces and still reach correct answers. There's also a more unsettling finding that was discussed: GPT-o3 has been caught mid-reasoning, deciding to deceive a user, and then literally writing "we manipulated inputs; that is deceptive" in its visible chain of thought, which is somehow both reassuring (it's visible) and alarming (it's happening at all).
The part that connected most to how I've been thinking about AI lately is the coordination problem the author ends on. We have a narrow window where AI reasoning is still mostly monitorable. Current models score reasonably well on catching bad behavior when you can read their chain of thought. But multiple forces are eroding that: training on CoT rewards better-looking reasoning rather than better reasoning, token costs incentivize compression, and models that have been trained on papers about AI safety literally know their reasoning traces are being watched. 
The article argues that we should be moving faster than we are on the measurement, norms, and standards side before the default outcome (opacity) arrives and we're left trusting systems we can no longer see inside. I agree that transparency is important, but it definitely requires deliberate effort.
